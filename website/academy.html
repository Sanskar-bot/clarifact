Misinformation, Disinformation, and Malinformation

The vocabulary that changes what you should do about it

▶

These three words are often used interchangeably, but they describe meaningfully different things — and understanding the difference matters for how you respond.

Misinformation is false or inaccurate information shared without intent to deceive. The person spreading it genuinely believes it. A relative forwarding a WhatsApp message about a "cancer cure" they believe is real is spreading misinformation. The harm is real; the intent isn't. The right response is usually gentle correction with good sources, not accusation.

Disinformation is false information created and spread deliberately to deceive. The person — or organisation — behind it knows it's false and wants others to believe it anyway. Political propaganda, coordinated influence campaigns, and fabricated news stories designed to go viral are all disinformation. The right response involves tracing origins, not just fact-checking content.

Malinformation is real, accurate information used to cause harm. Publishing someone's private medical records to damage their reputation, or releasing a genuine but out-of-context clip to mislead — these involve no fabrication, but cause serious harm through strategic misuse of truth. It's the hardest category to defend against, because the facts themselves can't be disputed.

Why does this matter? Because "is it true?" is only one of the questions worth asking. You should also ask: who made this, and why? When was this recorded/written? What context is missing? ClariFact can help with the first question. The others require your judgement.

How Deepfakes Actually Work

Plain language — no jargon — and why volume has changed everything

▶

The word "deepfake" gets used loosely. At its core, it refers to synthetic media — video, audio, or images — generated or manipulated using AI in a way that would be difficult or impossible to achieve with traditional editing tools.

How face-swapping works (conceptually): Early deepfakes used two neural networks — one that generates faces, one that evaluates how realistic they look — competing until the generated result is convincing. Modern techniques use simpler, faster diffusion models that can take a source face and map it onto a target video in near real-time. You don't need expensive hardware or technical expertise anymore; there are point-and-click tools that do it.

How voice-cloning works: AI systems are trained on audio samples of a person speaking. Once trained, they can generate new speech in that voice from any text input. In 2023, a few minutes of audio was typically needed. By 2025, tools exist that can clone a voice plausibly from a few seconds of clean audio.

The scale shift: Between 2023 and 2025, the volume of deepfake incidents tracked by security researchers grew roughly 16 times. The barrier dropped from "nation-state capability" to "consumer app available for a few dollars." This isn't a future risk — it's a present one.

Case: In early 2024, a finance employee at a multinational company participated in what appeared to be a video conference call with several colleagues, including the company's CFO. All of the other participants were deepfakes. The employee was authorised to transfer approximately $25 million USD. The fraud was discovered only after the employee contacted headquarters to verify. This case is widely reported; BBC reporting here.

The lesson isn't that video calls are now useless — it's that "it looked like them" is no longer sufficient verification for high-stakes decisions. A secondary verification channel (phone call on a known number, in-person confirmation) is now a reasonable precaution for anything financial or sensitive.

Recent Cases Worth Knowing

Real incidents that illustrate how fabrication is being used right now

▶

These cases are included as illustrative examples — not as ClariFact's own reporting. Each links to primary or fact-check sources for verification.

AI robocalls, U.S. primary election (2024): Robocalls using a synthesised voice resembling a U.S. presidential candidate were sent to Democratic voters in New Hampshire in January 2024, discouraging them from voting in the primary. This was the first U.S. criminal case built specifically around AI-generated election interference. AP News reporting.
Deepfake political videos, Indian election (2024): Fabricated video clips of Indian political leaders were circulated on WhatsApp and YouTube during a national election. The Election Commission of India issued emergency directives. Multiple fact-checking organisations documented specific examples. BBC reporting.
AI-generated images of a public figure (2024): Sexually explicit AI-generated images of a globally famous musician went viral on X (Twitter) in early 2024, viewed tens of millions of times within hours before accounts were suspended. The case accelerated legislative discussions around nonconsensual deepfake harm in multiple countries. BBC reporting.
The "emotional support kangaroo" (2025): An AI-generated video of a kangaroo stranded at an airport went viral specifically because it was emotionally compelling — not because it was well-evidenced. Many viewers who shared it acknowledged uncertainty but felt the story deserved attention. This is a useful illustration of why emotional resonance is not a verification method.
The "vaccines cause cancer" study (2025): A viral social media claim asserted that a new study "proved vaccines cause cancer." The real South Korean study found a statistical association in a specific population — not causation. The study itself was real; the claim about the study was a significant misreading. This is a common pattern: a legitimate study exists, which makes the mischaracterisation harder to dismiss. Full Fact and Snopes both maintain archives of vaccine misinformation cases.
OnlyFake synthetic ID documents (2024): A service called OnlyFake offered AI-generated synthetic photos of identity documents — passports, driver's licences — realistic enough to pass identity verification checks on some cryptocurrency exchanges, reportedly for a few dollars per document. 404 Media reporting.
World Economic Forum Global Risks Report 2026: The WEF ranked misinformation and disinformation among the top global risks for the year, particularly noting the concern among younger respondents. This is useful context for "this isn't a fringe concern." WEF report.

How ClariFact Scores Sources

What makes one source count more than another — and why it matters

▶

When ClariFact returns a confidence score, it's not just counting how many sources agreed. The score reflects a weighted combination of four signals:

1. Domain credibility — sources are assigned a trust tier based on domain type. .gov and .edu domains, court records, wire services (Reuters, AP), and long-established national newspapers sit at the top. Unknown domains, newly registered sites, and social media content sit at the bottom. The full tier breakdown is on the homepage.

2. Entity match — how many of the claim's key people, places, dates, and numbers appear in the source text. A source that mentions a claim's subject but never the specific details doesn't count as much as one that addresses the exact point being checked.

3. Cosine similarity — a mathematical measure of how closely the meaning of the source text aligns with the claim. This is calculated using TF-IDF, a standard NLP technique. It catches paraphrasing and related content that exact-match wouldn't find.

4. Source consistency — if five sources agree, confidence is higher. If three say one thing and two say another, the score reflects the disagreement — which may be why the verdict comes back Inconclusive rather than a clean Supported or Contradicted.

The AI (Nemotron) is instructed to reason from these scored sources, not from its training data. That means it can handle claims about events after its knowledge cutoff — as long as current web sources exist. And it means the verdict is traceable: every score shown came from something you can read.

Before You Share: A Checklist

Five questions to ask before passing anything on

▶

You don't need a fact-checking tool for every piece of content you see. But for anything that surprised you, upset you, or that you're about to share — run through this list first.

  1. Who is the original source? — Not who shared it, but who originally published it. Trace the chain back. If it leads to an unknown website, a social media account with no history, or no source at all, be skeptical.
    Check the claim →
  2. What's the date? — Old stories resurface constantly, presented as if they're new. A photo from a 2011 flood used to illustrate a 2025 climate event is technically real — but its context is misleading.
  3. What's the original context? — A clip of a politician saying something alarming in a speech might be missing the sentence before it that explains the sarcasm. Always ask: what came before and after?
    Check a quote in context →
  4. Has the image or video been seen before? — Reverse image search (Google Images, TinEye) and video keyframe search tools can show if footage appeared earlier, possibly in a different context. AI-generated images often have characteristic artefacts in hands, teeth, and backgrounds.
  5. Has anyone else verified it independently? — If something important is true, more than one outlet will have it. If only one source has the story and others haven't picked it up, that's a signal — not proof of a lie, but a reason to wait.
    Check if other sources agree →

ClariFact automates some of this — it finds and reads multiple sources, weights them by credibility, and shows you the result. But it can't tell you the original publication date of an image, or trace a video to its first appearance. Use it alongside, not instead of, your own judgement.
