How it works

Four steps, under five seconds.

Every check runs the same pipeline — search, read, score, judge. No black box.

Search

We query the web for sources related to your claim — news articles, government pages, Wikipedia, and more.

Read

The top results are fetched and their full article text extracted. Headlines and summaries aren't enough — we read the whole thing.

Score

Sources are scored for relevance and credibility. Government and academic sources rank higher than unknown blogs. A confidence score is calculated from the evidence.

Verdict

Nemotron (Nvidia's AI, running on AWS) reads the scored evidence and returns a structured verdict: Supported, Contradicted, or Inconclusive — with the reasoning shown.

Source credibility

Not all sources are equal.

ClariFact weights evidence by source credibility — so a .gov filing and a random opinion blog don't count the same.

TRUST 90–100%

Highest tier

Government sites (.gov), court records, peer-reviewed journals, established international wire services (Reuters, AP).

TRUST 70–89%

High tier

Major national newspapers, public broadcasters (BBC, NPR), Wikipedia (for established facts), academic institutions.

TRUST 50–69%

Mid tier

Regional news outlets, known specialty publications, large web encyclopedias — useful but weighted down.

TRUST <50%

Lower tier

Unknown blogs, social media content, personal sites — still included if they're the only source, but clearly weighted lower in the final score.

FAQ

Common questions.

No — and we want to be clear about that. ClariFact is a research aid, not an authority. It tells you what the top publicly available sources say about a claim, and how consistently they say it. For breaking news, niche topics, or anything where sources are limited or contradictory, the confidence score will be low. Always read the linked articles before drawing conclusions.

No. The backend is fully stateless — every request is independent, nothing is written to a database, and no logs are kept. Only the text you paste is sent to the server; no browsing history or page content is ever transmitted. You can verify this yourself: the backend source code is open on GitHub.

Standard LLMs answer from their training data, which has a cutoff date and may contain errors or biases. ClariFact searches the web live, fetches and reads current sources, and instructs the AI to reason only from what it found — not from prior knowledge. Every verdict comes with the sources it was based on, so you can check the AI's work.

It's a 0–100% score combining: how many sources were found, how credible those sources are (gov/academic rank higher), how closely the source text matches the claim's key entities, and how consistent the sources are with each other. A high score means many trustworthy sources agree. A low score means evidence is thin, mixed, or from less reliable sources.

Mostly yes. Web search and AI analysis work across many languages. Entity matching (identifying names, places, dates) is English-optimised, so confidence scores may be slightly lower for non-English claims. A note appears in results when this applies.

Yes. There's no subscription, no account, and no paywall. The project is funded by the owner's own time and hosting costs. To keep it free, there's a rate limit of 10 AI-powered checks per hour per IP address. The extension is also free to use.
